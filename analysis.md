1. Which city has the highest inefficiency ratio?
Sendai has the highest ratio at approximately 15× (1,581 ms measured vs. 105 ms theoretical). However, looking it up on submarinecablemap.com, Japan is one of the most well-connected countries in the world — served by dozens of transpacific cables. The extreme ratio is therefore not a routing or infrastructure problem. It's caused by the Tohoku University web server itself being slow to respond (heavy page, redirects, backend processing). This is confirmed by comparing it to Google's Tokyo target, which returned only 232 ms from a similar distance.
Among cities where routing is the primary bottleneck, Johannesburg (~14×) stands out. South Africa is served by cables like SAT-3/WASC, WACS, SAFE, and 2Africa, but none connect directly to North America — traffic must route through Europe first, then south along the entire African coast, greatly inflating the path length.

2. Which city is closest to the theoretical minimum?
Sydney (Google) at approximately 1.31× (212 ms measured vs. 162 ms theoretical). This reflects the high-quality transpacific cable infrastructure (Southern Cross, Hawaiki), strong peering at Sydney IX, and Google's local edge presence in Australia. At such a long distance (16,240 km), propagation delay dominates the total RTT, so fixed overheads like HTTP setup become proportionally negligible.

3. Why does Africa route through Europe, and what would need to change?
Lagos measured 213 ms with a ratio of ~2.6×. The packet almost certainly routes through Europe because all major submarine cables serving West Africa (SAT-3, ACE, WACS, GLO-1, Equiano) run northward along the coast to landing points in Portugal, France, or the UK. There is no direct cable from West Africa to North America, so traffic must detour through a European hub first.
This is compounded by a lack of local peering — African ISPs historically exchange traffic at European IXPs rather than locally, and most content is hosted in European data centers. To fix this, the region needs direct US–Africa cables, stronger regional IXPs (like IXPN Lagos and LINX Accra), local CDN/content deployment, and open-access cross-border terrestrial fiber. Projects like Google's Equiano and Meta's 2Africa cable are steps in this direction.
